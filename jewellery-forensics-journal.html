Terms Guide

Vocabulary that keeps coming up. Quick reference for reading a piece.

Alloy
A mixture of two or more metals. Precious metals are almost always alloyed for hardness, colour, and workability — pure gold is too soft to wear.
Annealing
Heat treatment that relieves work hardening, restoring malleability. A fabricator may anneal a piece dozens of times. Quench in water or allow to air-cool.
Bearing
The ledge or seat inside a setting on which a gemstone rests. Must match the stone's girdle diameter precisely — too wide = wobble, too narrow = stone sits too high.
Bezel
A continuous strip of metal encircling a gemstone. Can be full (360°) or partial. Set by burnishing or pressing metal over the stone's girdle.
Bright cut
Engraving technique removing metal at a sharp angle to create faceted, light-catching walls — used extensively in channel and pavé settings.
Burnishing
Polishing by compression, not abrasion. A hard tool pushed across the metal closes grain, increases reflectivity, and pushes bezels over stones.
Casting porosity
Gas bubbles or shrinkage voids trapped during cooling. Appears as pitting under magnification — most visible near sprues and thick sections.
Chasing
Shaping or decorating metal from the front with hammers and punches. Companion technique to repoussé, which works from the back.
Die striking
Forming metal by pressing sheet between two hardened steel dies under high pressure. Produces very dense, work-hardened, crisply detailed pieces.
Ductility
Ability to be drawn into wire without breaking. Gold is highly ductile — 1 g can be drawn into roughly 3 km of wire.
Electroforming
Depositing metal electrolytically onto a mandrel (which is then removed), producing hollow, seamless, very lightweight pieces with thin walls.
Fabrication
Building jewellery from sheet, wire, and tube by cutting, forming, and soldering — as opposed to casting from molten metal.
Fine metal
Unalloyed, pure metal — fine gold (999.9‰), fine silver (999‰). Too soft for most wearable jewellery without alloying.
Fineness mark
A stamp indicating metal purity in parts per thousand — 750 = 18K gold, 925 = sterling silver, 950 = platinum. Required by law in many countries.
Fire scale
A reddish-purple stain from copper oxides forming below the surface of copper-bearing alloys during heating. Common in rose gold and sterling silver.
Flux
Chemical applied before soldering to prevent oxidation and encourage solder flow. Borax is traditional; commercial paste fluxes are more common today.
Gallery
The underside or interior of a piece — the first place to examine, as it's harder to polish and more likely to show construction evidence.
Grain (crystal)
Microscopic crystalline structures in metal alloys. Fine-grained = stronger. Coarsened by overheating; refined by alloying and mechanical working.
Hallmark
An officially assayed and guaranteed purity stamp applied by an assay office. The UK has required hallmarking since 1300. Systems vary by country.
Hardness (HV)
Resistance to permanent deformation, measured on the Vickers scale. Pure gold ~25 HV; 14K yellow ~130–180 HV; platinum alloys ~40–120 HV depending on alloy.
Heft
Perceived weight relative to size — a key field-identification clue. Gold (19.3 g/cm³) and platinum (21.5 g/cm³) are noticeably denser than silver (10.5) or base metals.
Karat (K)
US measure of gold purity. 24K = pure (999.9‰). 18K = 75% gold (750‰). 14K = 58.3% (585‰). 10K = 41.7% (417‰) — legal minimum in the USA.
Lost-wax casting
A wax model is embedded in investment, burned out in a kiln, and replaced with molten metal forced in by centrifuge or vacuum. The dominant production method.
Malleability
Ability to be hammered flat without cracking. Gold is the most malleable element — 1 g can be beaten into a 1 m² sheet less than 0.1 µm thick.
Mokumé-gané
Japanese technique bonding dissimilar metal layers under heat and pressure, then carving or rolling to reveal a wood-grain or contour pattern. Originated 17th-century Japan.
Pavé
Setting style in which gemstones are closely packed and secured by tiny raised metal grains, with minimal metal visible between stones. French for "paved."
Prong (claw)
A thin metal projection gripping a gemstone. Should extend 1/3 to 1/2 up the crown. Worn prongs are the primary cause of stone loss — key inspection point.
Repoussé
Decorating metal from the back with hammer and punches to create raised relief on the front. Combined with chasing (front work) for fine detail.
Rhodium plating
Electroplating with rhodium — a bright, hard platinum-group metal. Applied over white gold and silver for tarnish resistance and whiteness. Wears off with use.
Solder
A lower-melting alloy used to join metal components. In gold work, solder karatage must match the piece. Classified as hard, medium, or easy by flow temperature.
Sprue
The channel through which metal flows into a mould during casting. Leaves a stub or filled depression on the finished piece — a reliable indicator of casting.
Work hardening
Increased hardness and brittleness caused by mechanical deformation compressing the crystal grain structure. Every bend, draw, or strike hardens metal. Reversed by annealing.

Manufacturing Methods & Telltale Signs

How to identify the manufacturing process from what the piece shows you. Most pieces combine methods.

Method Identification Guide

Lost-Wax Casting
Wax model → investment mould → metal injected by centrifuge or vacuum. The most common production method for complex forms.
  • Porosity (pitting) visible at 10× — especially near thick sections
  • Sprue mark or filled depression on underside
  • Soft, slightly blurred fine detail compared to die-struck
  • Uniform granular texture in unpolished recesses
  • Complex undercuts and full 3D forms possible
  • Parting line if a two-part mould was used
CAD/CAM → Cast
3D-printed wax or resin model replaces hand-carved wax, then cast by lost-wax. Increasingly the default for commercial production.
  • Step lines / growth rings on curved surfaces (print layer artefacts)
  • Geometrically perfect, often uncannily symmetrical forms
  • Very fine, perfectly consistent repeat detail (prong spacing, pattern)
  • Still shows casting porosity — it's still cast
  • No tool marks, no organic variation whatsoever
  • Sprue mark still present
High-end vs mass-market: both use CAD — finishing quality is where they diverge
Hand Fabrication
Metal sawn, formed, and joined from sheet, wire, and tube by a bench jeweller. The traditional craft method; often combined with casting.
  • File marks on inner curves, edges, and back surfaces
  • Solder seams visible at 10× — possible colour or texture mismatch
  • Slight asymmetry — human variation in repeated elements
  • Hammer or planishing marks in hand-worked areas
  • Fire scale on copper-alloy metals near joins
  • No porosity in the fabricated elements (no casting)
Die Striking
Sheet metal pressed between hardened steel dies under high tonnage. Used for medals, coins, and high-volume repeat elements.
  • Extremely crisp, sharp edges — far crisper than cast
  • Work-hardened density — surprisingly heavy for apparent volume
  • Zero porosity
  • Thin, uniform wall thickness throughout
  • Often hollow or open back
  • Very faint parting seam where dies meet
  • Surfaces can be mirror-smooth without extra polishing
Machining (CNC / Lathe)
Metal cut away by rotating tools to achieve precise geometry. Common for shanks, tube settings, and findings.
  • Perfectly concentric circles (lathe work)
  • Parallel spiral tool marks inside bores or channels
  • Extremely precise, repeatable dimensions
  • Sharp internal corners that casting cannot achieve
  • No porosity, no solder seams, no parting lines
  • Dense, solid feel throughout
Electroforming
Metal deposited electrolytically onto a conductive mandrel, which is then dissolved or removed. Produces hollow, seamless pieces.
  • Extremely lightweight for apparent size — the defining tell
  • Hollow interior — tap gently for a hollow sound
  • No solder seams anywhere on the body
  • Very thin walls — vulnerable to denting under pressure
  • Smooth, slightly grainy interior surface
  • No sprue marks, no parting lines
Easy to confuse with casting — weight and wall thickness are the giveaway
Mokumé-gané
Dissimilar metal layers fused under heat and pressure, carved or rolled to reveal a wood-grain or contour pattern within the metal.
  • Wood-grain or wavy pattern is in the metal — not applied or printed
  • Pattern continues through cut edges (laminated layers visible in section)
  • Subtle colour variation shifts across the surface
  • Always unique — no two pieces have identical patterning
  • May show hammer or rolling marks at the edges
Repoussé & Chasing
Relief decoration hammered from the back (repoussé) and refined from the front (chasing). One of the oldest metalworking techniques.
  • Raised relief on front, corresponding hollow on reverse
  • Punch and tool impressions visible in unfinished recesses
  • Organic, slightly irregular quality to relief forms
  • Thin metal — stretched and displaced from a single sheet
  • No porosity, no sprue marks on the worked element
  • Possible pitch residue traces on reverse (pitch bowl backing)

Era Dating Guide — Manufacturing Clues by Period

Georgian
1714–1837
Hand fabrication · Repoussé · Filigree · Granulation · Engraving · Cannetille
Foil-backed closed settings (no light through stones). Pinchbeck (copper-zinc gold substitute). Cut-steel. Paste stones. Mourning jewellery with woven hair.
→ C-catch on brooches (no rollover safety). Closed-back bezels. Very fine hand engraving. No machine-made elements whatsoever.
Early Victorian
1837–1861
Hand fabrication · Repoussé · Enamel · Granulation revival (Castellani)
Naturalistic motifs: flowers, leaves, serpents. Sentimental: hair lockets, mourning pieces. Archaeological revival begins. Yellow gold dominates.
→ C-catch or early trombone catch. All yellow gold. Serpent and floral motifs. Hand-engraved surfaces throughout.
Grand Victorian
1861–1885
Repoussé · Early die striking · Jet & bog oak carving · Scottish pebble work
Large scale, bold forms. Mourning jewellery peaked after Prince Albert's death (1861). Archaeological (Roman, Egyptian, Etruscan) revival. Scottish hardstones.
→ Jet, whitby jet, bog oak, black glass (onyx substitute). Heavier, larger pieces. Machine-cut elements beginning to appear alongside handwork.
Late Victorian
1885–1901
Die striking grows · Early casting machines · Electroforming · Japanese influence
Lighter, more delicate forms. Japanese motifs (fans, insects, cherry blossom). Rose-cut diamonds. Silver with demantoid garnet, turquoise, seed pearl. Rolled gold (gold-filled) becomes common.
→ T-bar and toggle clasps. Early box catches. Rolled gold marking. Some machine uniformity — look for slight inconsistency that suggests it isn't fully hand-made.
Art Nouveau
1890–1910
Hand fabrication · Plique-à-jour enamel · Repoussé · Horn & ivory carving
Organic flowing lines. Female figures, dragonflies, orchids, peacocks. Unusual stones: opals, moonstones, baroque pearls. Enamel used as primary colour medium, not just accent.
→ Plique-à-jour enamel is nearly diagnostic. Asymmetric organic forms. Fine hand-fabricated construction throughout.
Edwardian
1901–1915
Platinum for settings · Millegrain · Filigree · Knife-edge shanks · Open basket settings
White-on-white look: platinum + diamonds + white pearls. Lacy open metalwork possible only in platinum (too fragile in gold). Garland style, bow and ribbon motifs.
→ Platinum hallmarks (Pt 850 or 950). Millegrain bead edges. Safety catches on brooches (introduced ~1901). Very open, almost lace-like setting structures.
Art Deco
1920–1940
Precision machining · Calibré-cut stones · Platinum dominant · Pavé · Baguette cuts
Geometric precision. Black & white contrasts (onyx + diamond). Step cuts (emerald, baguette, French cut). Bold colour combinations ("Tutti Frutti": sapphire + emerald + ruby carved by Cartier).
→ Calibré-cut coloured stones in geometric patterns is nearly diagnostic. Platinum + geometric = almost certainly Deco or Edwardian. Millegrain + platinum + step cuts = strong Deco.
Retro / Mid-Century
1940–1965
Yellow gold dominant · Mass casting begins (rubber mold vulcanization 1940s) · Synthetic stones
Bold dimensional forms. Heavy yellow or rose gold. Hardware-inspired: padlocks, chains, scrolls. Platinum restricted in USA 1942–1945 (strategic war material). Large synthetic sapphires and rubies.
→ Yellow/rose gold in bold sculptural forms = possibly wartime or post-war. Chunky link bracelets. Box clasps with safety tongue. Early casting sprue marks visible on underside.
Bold Gold Era
1970s–1980s
Electroforming revival · Granulation revival · Mokumé introduced to West · Italian design
Large hollow gold pieces (electroformed). Italian design dominance (Bulgari, Pomellato). Textured and matte finishes (florentine, bark). Mixed metal work. Studio jewellery movement.
→ Hollow lightweight gold = likely electroformed 1970s–80s. Tubular link designs. Matte florentine or bark textures. Early Western mokumé-gané in art jewellery contexts.
CAD / Contemporary
1997–present
CAD/CAM → casting · Laser welding · Laser engraving · CNC machining · Tension settings
Geometrically perfect forms. Complex pavé arrangements previously impossible by hand. Tension settings. Branded pieces with laser-marked logos. Minimal bar and bezel styles.
→ Perfect bilateral symmetry in all details = likely CAD. Laser-stamped hallmarks (crisp, uniform depth). Step-line artefacts on curved surfaces = 3D print origin. Complex pavé with perfectly equal spacing throughout.

Examination Protocol — Research Notes

GIA standard examination magnification is 10×, the same used for diamond clarity grading. At this power, casting porosity, 3D printing grow lines, solder colour mismatches, and tool marks all become visible. Start from the back/underside (the gallery), then move to edges, joints, and finally the front — prevents being "sold" by the polished presentation before reading the construction story.

Fiber-optic illumination at an oblique angle reveals surface texture that overhead lighting hides. Professional microscopes go to 40×–60× for detailed surface analysis.

Quality Assurance Benchmarks

First step with a finished piece is QA. Looking for inconsistencies, pits, solder seams. "We look at edges, underside, corners — stuff that was hard to hide or perfect."

✓ Well-Executed

  • Removal of evidence of manufacture
  • Complete removal of tool markings
  • Crisp sharp straight edges
  • Secure stones
  • Functioning mechanics

✗ Poorly Executed

  • Increased risk of damage
  • Higher likelihood of repairs
  • Gemstone loss
  • Mechanical failure
  • Lower value, possible lawsuit
"Can I sleep wearing this?" — debate, nuance. But in theory if it's really well done, it should be safe.

Handling: White gloves policy — for polish. But don't overpolish. Workmanship has an endless skill ceiling. "Humans have been making jewellery for thousands and thousands of years."

Historical Context

The earliest decorative jewellery consists of shell beads dating back ~100,000 years (Blombos Cave, South Africa). Gold jewellery has been crafted since at least 4,500 BCE — the Varna Necropolis in Bulgaria contains the oldest known gold artifacts. Gold's inability to corrode or tarnish explains why ancient pieces survive in pristine condition — they truly "come out of the ground in the same condition they went in."

Gold Alloys — Deeper Properties

Rose gold's high copper content makes it not only brittle but prone to fire scale (a reddish-purple stain from copper oxide forming beneath the surface during heating). It can also crack during ring sizing. Palladium white gold, introduced widely in the early 2000s, solved the nickel allergy problem that affected ~10–15% of wearers — EU regulations (the Nickel Directive, 1994) restricted nickel in jewellery contacting skin, driving the shift.

Work hardening is key to understanding alloys: every bend, strike, or draw compresses the crystal grain, making metal harder and more brittle. Annealing (heating to a specific temperature, then cooling) resets the structure. A fabricator may anneal a piece dozens of times during construction.

Hallmarking — A Deeper Look

Hallmarks are common outside the US — not done by the jeweller but by an independent assay office (from the notes). European brands have strict hallmarking.

Hallmarking is one of the world's oldest consumer protections, dating to King Louis IX of France and Edward I of England in the 1200s. In 1327, Edward III chartered the Worshipful Company of Goldsmiths at Goldsmiths' Hall, London — origin of the word "hallmark."

Platinum

In 1939, designated a strategic metal. Notoriously difficult to work with — the test of a jeweller's mettle.

"When the Spanish conquistadors arrived in Colombia they found platinum. They tried to melt it — it wouldn't melt. Tried to form it — too hard. Didn't polish right. They called it 'Platina' — little silver, inferior silver."

Didn't figure out working with it until 1870s. "Oh it's actually great."

Platinum — Extended History & Properties

The conquistadors threw platinum back into rivers, believing it was "unripe gold." Meanwhile, the La Tolita people (Ecuador/Colombia, ~600 BCE–400 CE) had been working platinum for centuries using sintering techniques Europe wouldn't rediscover for 2,000 years. Earliest known Pt use: Egyptian hieroglyph decorations ~700 BCE using Pt-Au alloy.

Key breakthrough: 1895, Fouché and Picard invent the oxyacetylene torch, finally reaching Pt's 1,768°C melting point. Louis Cartier pioneers Pt jewellery — delicate "Garland Style" diamond settings impossible in gold. In WWII, Pt was prohibited for civilian use; white gold was invented as its substitute (Pforzheim, Germany, 1912).

Forensic note: Platinum doesn't wear away — it displaces. Scratches push metal aside rather than removing it. Pt develops a characteristic matte patina. All Pt jewellery is 90–95% pure, vs. gold which can be as low as 37.5% (9K).

Manufacturing Methods

Covering 5–7 thousand years. "If I have done my job well nobody can tell how I made it." The CAD designer is one half, but the jeweller makes a big difference too — an interesting dichotomy/duo.

Market considerations: cost, metal materials (some alloys prefer different methods), complexity, reproducibility, speed, durability, comfort. All tools have finite life — expensive specialized equipment contributes to cost and preciousness.

Lost Wax Casting Casting

Wax model is carved → mould is made → wax melted out → metal poured in. Master model → multiple moulds → each makes new wax → more moulds. Shrinks ~3%. Lost Wax Casting — Historical & Technical Background

The oldest known lost-wax-cast objects date to ~4550 BCE (Varna Necropolis, Bulgaria). Also called cire perdue (French) or investment casting. Used across Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Benin Kingdom (the famous Benin Bronzes), and pre-Columbian Americas.

Modern breakthrough: 1936, Danish engineer Thoger Jungersen patented the rubber mould, enabling multiple wax copies from one master. Before this, every wax was destroyed — a one-shot process. Today, waxes are typically injected into rubber moulds, attached to a central "tree" (sprue), invested in plaster, and burnt out at 700–760°C.

Cast pieces are less dense than die-struck or fabricated equivalents — they wear faster, don't hold as high a polish. But casting offers nearly unlimited design freedom: undercuts, organic shapes, complex 3D forms impossible via other methods.

CAD/CAM & 3D Printing Modern Casting

CAD makes mirror images easy, gives access to digital design libraries, accuracy to 0.1mm. Got really "CADy" at first — angular, architectural. Now out of vogue. CNC wax carvers: slow, expensive. 3D printing has largely replaced them.

Telltale Signs of 3D Printing

  • Grow linesLayer lines from the build process, each 25–100 microns thick. Like tree growth rings. Look in nooks/crannies where polishing doesn't reach. — built layer by layer, "like the lines of a tree." Look in nooks and crannies
  • Support nubs — models hang upside down from a resin bathSLA/DLP 3D printers build models upside-down by selectively curing a vat of UV-sensitive resin. Supports hold the model to the build plate. The resulting castable pattern replaces hand-carved wax.. Sometimes leave small marks. Usually cleaned up
  • Perfect bilateral symmetry — digital mirror tools
  • Uniform wall thickness — mathematically precise

Modern CAD/CAM Workflow

Industry-standard software: Rhino + MatrixGold (formerly Matrix), ZBrush for organic forms, JewelCAD. Modern SLA/DLP printers achieve 25-micron layer resolution — what once took a skilled carver days prints overnight. Direct Metal Laser Sintering (DMLS) is emerging: metal powder sintered by laser, skipping wax entirely. Still rare in fine jewellery but growing for titanium/steel.

Why Hand Fabrication Produces Superior Metal

Starts from wrought metal — rolled/drawn from ingot, compressing the grain structure. This produces the densest, strongest jewellery possible. Edges are crisper, metal takes a higher polish, and work can survive centuries. The aesthetic advantage: solder joints are sharp, angles crisp, wires retain perfect cylindrical shape. Custom hand-fabricated pieces bear the full cost of the process — always more expensive than comparable mass-produced items.

CNC Machining in Jewellery

Typically uses Swiss-type lathes or 5-axis CNC mills. Metal starts as solid wrought stock, so machined pieces share the density of hand-fabricated work. Main limitation: subtractive process — no undercuts or hollow forms without secondary operations. Tolerances to ±0.01mm. Mokume-Gane

Mokume-GanePronounced MOH-koo-may GAH-nay. 木目金 = "wood grain metal." Japanese laminate technique: layer metals, carve to different depths, roll flat to reveal woodgrain pattern. — Japanese technique for laminating different coloured metals into a billet, then drilling/carving indentations to different depths, then rolling out to produce a woodgrain pattern.

Mokume-Gane — History & Craft

Invented ~1660 by Denbei Shoami (Akita Prefecture) for samurai sword fittings. Originally called "guri bori" after Chinese carved lacquerwork. Traditional metals: gold, silver, copper, shakudō (gold-copper, dark purple), shibuichi (silver-copper, grey-green). Tiffany & Co. experimented ~1877. Nearly extinct by mid-20th century; revived in 1970s by Hiroko Sato Pijanowski. Today popular for wedding bands. ~65% of metal weight is lost as waste. Every piece is unique.

Solder

The filler metal. Signs of solder: porosity at the jointBubbles/holes at a solder joint = overheating, dirt/oil contamination, or improper fit between parts. = overheating, dirt, or improper fit.

Good joints: colour match, proper heat, no excess, no pitting. Bad joints: gloppy, pitting, incomplete flow. Check bracelets: are the links even soldered? If not, not durable.

Lead Solder ⚠

  • Dark grey seam. Never a good colour match
  • Risky to remove — leaches into goldLead diffuses into gold's crystal structure during application. Even after removing visible lead, microscopic contamination remains — permanently compromising the surrounding metal.
  • Test: scratch with a pin — if it burnishesLead is so soft that light pressure from a pin will smooth and shine its surface. If a grey solder joint polishes up almost effortlessly, it's likely lead. with almost no pressure, it's lead
  • Common in late 1800s–early 1900s. Reduces value

Laser Welding — The Modern Replacement

"As jewellers leap into the 21st century... we use lasers."
  • Joins metal without solder — a true weld. Box about desk-sized
  • So precise you can weld near sensitive gems (amethyst, coral, pearl)
  • Looks like a "roll of dimes pushed to one side"
  • No colour difference — no filler material. If cleaned properly, invisible
  • Can use tiny gold wire held against weld point for extra material
  • Platinum reacts very nicely with laser — can polish and remove porosity
Platinum repairs often done with white gold solder — Pt needs such high temp that diamonds would burn (~850°C).

Solder Types — Technical Detail

Gold/silver solders come in temperature grades: Hard (~745°C for silver), Medium (~720°C), Easy (~680°C). Using them in sequence (hard first, then medium, then easy) allows multiple joints without melting earlier work. Flux (borax-based) prevents oxidation during heating — residual flux appears as glassy residue in crevices (another forensic clue).

Laser welding works by focusing a pulsed laser beam (typically Nd:YAG, wavelength 1064nm) to a spot ~0.5mm diameter. Energy is absorbed so locally that surrounding metal barely heats — enabling work millimeters from heat-sensitive stones. The "roll of dimes" appearance is from overlapping circular weld spots.

GranulationAncient practice (Etruscan, ~700 BCE) bonding tiny gold beads to surface without visible solder. Original method used copper salt + hide glue for colloidal bonding. True granulation has air gaps between beads; imitations are cast/soldered (gaps filled).

Ancient practice of bonding tiny beads to the surface. Real granulation = beads bonded (not cast or soldered) — with little air gaps.

Granulation & Etruscan Metalwork

The Etruscans (~700–300 BCE, central Italy) mastered granulation with beads as small as 0.14mm diameter — a technique so refined that it was lost for over 2,000 years. The secret involved copper salt + organic glue: when heated, the glue burned away and the copper reduced, creating a thin fusion bond between bead and surface without melting either. Modern goldsmith John Paul Miller (1918–2010) rediscovered the technique in the 1940s through years of experimentation.

Enamel — Further Context

Enamel is essentially glass fused to metal. The base glass (flux) is mixed with metal oxides for colour: cobalt = blue, copper = green/turquoise, gold chloride = ruby red, iron = brown/black. Firing temperatures and kiln atmosphere affect the final hue. Enamel is brittle and can chip on impact — a key vulnerability for enamelled pieces.

René Lalique (1860–1945) revolutionized Art Nouveau jewellery by prioritizing enamel, glass, and horn over diamonds and platinum. His plique-à-jour work remains among the most technically accomplished ever produced. Peter Carl Fabergé (1846–1920) employed over 500 craftsmen and mastered guilloche enamel (engine-turned metal under translucent enamel) — over 140 distinct enamel colours.

Settings — Further Reference

The invisible setting (serti invisible/Mystery Set) was actually patented by Van Cleef & Arpels in 1933 — not Cartier. The technique requires stones cut with precise grooves (typically 0.3mm deep) that slide onto a thin metal rail framework. Setting a single piece can take hundreds of hours. The original patent has expired, but VCA remains the acknowledged master.

Setting choice impacts insurance and maintenance: prong settings need inspection every 6–12 months (prong retipping is the most common repair). Bezel settings rarely need maintenance but make stones appear slightly smaller. Channel settings are lowest-maintenance for daily wear.

Identifying repairs: colour mismatch at solder joints, different solder grades at different times, irregular prong heights, areas of different surface texture (new polish vs. aged patina).

Tennis bracelet trick: turn it upside down to see if it hangs with any kinks — each kink = a repair point. Then inspect that spot closely.

Faux antique: Look for 3D printing grow lines, modern milling marks, or CAD-precise symmetry on pieces styled to look vintage. "Looking at the lowground to see what tools were used and how."

Repair Red Flags — Forensic Checklist

  • Solder colour mismatch — different karats of solder used at different times
  • Excess solder blobs — especially in hard-to-reach areas
  • Asymmetric prong heights — some retipped, some original
  • Mismatched surface finish — newly polished area vs. aged patina
  • Lead solder (grey, burnishes easily) — reduces value, difficult to remediate
  • Laser weld spots — "roll of dimes" pattern under magnification
  • Sizing evidence — visible join on shank interior, thickness variation
  • Replaced stones — slight colour/cut differences from original set
